Introduction¶

Dataset Description¶

The dataset used in this project is the TMDb Movie Dataset, which contains information about thousands of movies collected from The Movie Database (TMDb). The dataset includes various attributes such as movie title, genres, popularity, budget, revenue, vote count, vote average, directors, cast members, and release dates.

These variables allow us to explore different aspects of movies such as financial performance, audience engagement, and popularity. The dataset provides useful information for analyzing movie success and identifying patterns between genres, directors, and profitability.

Question(s) for Analysis¶

  1. Which movie genre generates the highest average profit?
  2. Who are the most successful directors?

# Reusable function to split multi-value columns and expand them into separate rows
def split_and_explode(dataframe, column):
    df_copy = dataframe.copy()
    df_copy[column] = df_copy[column].str.split('|')
    df_copy = df_copy.explode(column)
    return df_copy

Conclusions¶

This analysis investigated the TMDb movie dataset to answer two research questions.

The first question asked which movie genre generates the highest average profit. The results showed that Animation is the most profitable genre on average. This genre also tends to have high adjusted budgets, strong adjusted revenues, and good popularity values, which may help explain its strong financial performance.

The second question focused on the most successful directors based on vote count, vote average, and popularity. The analysis showed that Christopher Nolan is one of the most successful directors in the dataset, as his movies consistently combine high audience engagement, strong popularity, and high average ratings , profit was not used alone to identify the most successful director because some directors appeared only once in the dataset.

One important limitation of this analysis is that many movies with zero or missing budget and revenue values had to be removed, which reduced the dataset size and may affect the generalizability of the findings. In addition, this analysis is exploratory only, and no statistical tests were performed. Therefore, the results should be interpreted as descriptive patterns and not as evidence of causation.
